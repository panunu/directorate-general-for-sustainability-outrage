## Explanatory memorandum

The ability to express one's individuality is one of the keystones of Union citizens' rights. EU citizens accordingly **retain the freedom either to modify the exhaust system of their motor vehicle in order to deliberately increase its inherent volume, or to install additional sound-producing equipment** such as subwoofers and aftermarket amplifiers.

To promote awareness of the effects of such activity, which shall not be condemned, this Regulation requires that **vehicle owners install, at their place of residence (or, in cases where the natural person resides alone, at the place of residence of a family member designated by the national authority), an audio reproduction system capable of replicating, in real time and at the correct sound-pressure level, any external sound emitted by the operated vehicle**.

The Regulation reframes the externalities of personal-vehicle sound emission as a matter of household acoustic responsibility, ensuring that the parties most able to influence the operator's behaviour (those sharing a roof with the operator) are exposed to the resulting soundscape on equal terms with the wider community.

## Recitals

> *(1)* The freedom of self-expression, recognised in Article 11 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union, extends in modern Union life to the manner and volume in which natural persons present their motorised conveyances to the wider community. This Regulation does not restrict that freedom.
>
> *(2)* Persons electing to modify the acoustic profile of their motor vehicle bear a smaller share of the resulting acoustic environment than the residents, pedestrians, and children incidentally exposed to it. This asymmetry between the producer of an externality and the parties bearing its consequences is the principal market failure addressed by this Regulation.
>
> *(3)* Empirical evidence from related fields suggests that the parties most able to influence an individual's behaviour are those sharing the individual's place of residence. Reproducing, within the household, the acoustic output of the operated vehicle aligns the experience of the household with that of the wider community and ensures that behavioural feedback flows from the most proximate parties.
>
> *(4)* This Regulation does not restrict the freedom of the operator to modify the vehicle, to install additional sound-producing equipment, or to operate the vehicle at any volume. It confines itself to the passive and symmetrical reproduction of the resulting sound to the operator and the operator's household, and accordingly satisfies the principle of proportionality enshrined in Article 5(4) of the Treaty on European Union.

## In simple English

:::plain-english
Cars and other motor vehicles often produce noises that can be disturbing, especially in cities. Some drivers purposefully make those noises even louder. To raise awareness and to possibly reduce the volume caused by vehicles, any external sounds produced by any vehicle will be reproduced inside the driver's home – or, in case the driver lives alone, in a relative's home.

## Scientific basis

Environmental noise is the second-largest environmental cause of ill-health in the European Union, after airborne particulate matter. Long-term exposure to traffic, rail, and aircraft noise produces measurable physiological harm distinct from auditory damage. The principal mechanisms are sleep fragmentation, sympathetic-nervous-system activation, oxidative stress, and chronic stress-axis dysregulation; the principal end-points include ischaemic heart disease, hypertension, stroke, type 2 diabetes, cognitive impairment in children, and mental-health outcomes such as depression and anxiety.

Quantitatively, the European Environment Agency estimates that long-term exposure to environmental noise contributes annually to **12 000 premature deaths** and **48 000 new cases of ischaemic heart disease** across European Environment Agency member countries. The World Health Organization assigns environmental noise approximately **1 million Disability-Adjusted Life Years** lost per year in the European Region — second only to fine particulate matter among environmental risk factors.

The current WHO *Environmental Noise Guidelines for the European Region* (2018) recommend that average road-traffic noise should not exceed **53 dB Lden** during the day and **45 dB Lnight** at night for the protection of public health. More than **20 %** of the Union's population lives in areas where these values are exceeded. Aftermarket exhaust modifications, high-output amplifiers, and subwoofers used in motor vehicles directly contribute to this exceedance, with peak emissions reaching **95–110 dB(A)** at the residential property line.

The youth is, again, disproportionately affected. School-age children chronically exposed to traffic noise show measurable deficits in reading comprehension and long-term memory, with delays in reading age associated with each 5 dB increase in chronic exposure. Sleep disturbance is dose-responsive from night-time exposures of approximately **40 dB / night** upward, and chronic sleep fragmentation in turn mediates much of the cardiovascular and metabolic effect.

These findings are not contested in the peer-reviewed literature. Reducing noise at source — through tighter limits on vehicle pass-by noise (UNECE R51.03), lower urban speed limits, the removal of high-emission aftermarket modifications, and quiet road surfaces — is consistently identified as the most effective intervention.
